Chapter:
0.1
0.1 — Functions
Function notation · domain · range · piecewise · difference quotient
▾
Must be able to do
  • Evaluate f(a), f(x+h), f(g(x))
  • Find domain of radical and rational functions
  • Simplify [f(x+h)−f(x)]/h fully before canceling h
Foundation of all calculus. The difference quotient becomes the derivative definition in 2.2.
Common mistakes
  • Not canceling h after expanding the difference quotient
  • Wrong domain: √x requires x≥0, ln x requires x>0
  • Confusing f(x+h) with f(x)+h
Write out f(x+h) fully by substituting (x+h) everywhere x appears. Then subtract f(x) and simplify before touching h.
0.2
0.2 — Transformations
Shifts · stretches · reflections · composition of functions
▾
Must be able to do
  • Graph y=−2f(x−3)+1 from f(x)
  • Compute (f∘g)(x) and determine its domain
  • Distinguish f(g(x)) from g(f(x))
Composition is the structure behind the Chain Rule (2.6). Transformations reappear in curve sketching (4.1–4.3).
Common mistakes
  • Horizontal shift direction: x−3 shifts RIGHT, not left
  • Applying vertical and horizontal changes in wrong order
  • Forgetting domain restrictions in composition
For composition: work inside-out — evaluate g(x) first, then feed result into f.
0.4
0.4 — Inverse Functions
One-to-one · finding f⁻¹(x) · domain/range swap · inverse trig principal ranges
▾
Must be able to do
  • Find f⁻¹(x) algebraically by swapping x and y
  • Apply horizontal line test
  • State principal ranges: arcsin [−π/2,π/2], arccos [0,π], arctan (−π/2,π/2)
Needed for implicit differentiation (3.1) and inverse trig derivatives (3.3).
Common mistakes
  • Confusing f⁻¹(x) with 1/f(x)
  • Forgetting to restrict domain before inverting
  • Wrong principal range for arccos — it is [0,π] not [−π/2,π/2]
To find f⁻¹: replace f(x) with y, swap x and y, solve for y. Check domain restriction first.
0.5
0.5 — Exponential & Log Functions
Log laws · e^x and ln x as inverses · solving equations
▾
Must be able to do
  • Solve ln(x−1)+ln(x+2)=ln10 (check for extraneous solutions)
  • Simplify e^(ln x)=x and ln(e^x)=x instantly
  • Convert between log bases and sketch e^x and ln x
e^x and ln x appear throughout Early Transcendentals. Needed for log derivatives (3.2) and exp derivatives (3.3).
Common mistakes
  • Treating ln(a+b) as ln a + ln b — this is WRONG
  • Not checking for extraneous solutions after solving log equations
  • Forgetting domain restriction x>0 for ln x
For log equations: combine logs using laws, exponentiate both sides. Always check solutions in the original equation.
1.2
1.2 — Computing Limits
Direct substitution · 0/0 form · factoring · rationalizing · Squeeze Theorem
▾
Must be able to do
  • Recognize 0/0 and know it needs more algebra
  • Factor and cancel polynomial limits
  • Rationalize conjugates: (√a−√b)(√a+√b)=a−b
  • Know lim sin(x)/x = 1 and lim (1−cos x)/x = 0 as x→0
The derivative definition in 2.2 is itself a 0/0 form: [f(x+h)−f(x)]/h as h→0.
Common mistakes
  • Writing lim sin(x)/x = 0 instead of 1 — extremely common error
  • Canceling before fully factoring
  • Sign errors in conjugate expansion
Step 1: always try direct substitution. If 0/0 — stop. Factor or rationalize. Cancel. Substitute again.
1.5
1.5 — Continuity
Three conditions · discontinuity types · IVT · piecewise continuity
▾
Must be able to do
  • Check all 3 conditions at a specific point
  • Classify: removable (hole), jump, infinite discontinuity
  • Find k so that piecewise function is continuous
  • State and apply IVT to prove a root exists
Continuity is required for EVT (4.4), MVT (4.8), and FTC (5.6). Non-negotiable.
Common mistakes
  • Checking only one or two of the three conditions
  • Saying continuous because limit exists — limit must also EQUAL f(a)
  • Applying IVT without verifying continuity on the closed interval
For piecewise: at each boundary compute left limit, right limit, and f(boundary) separately. All three must match.
2.3–2.4
2.3–2.4 — Differentiation Rules
Power rule · product rule · quotient rule
▾
Must be able to do
  • Differentiate any polynomial instantly
  • Rewrite √x as x^(1/2), 1/x as x^(−1) before power rule
  • Apply product rule: d/dx[fg]=f'g+fg'
  • Apply quotient rule: d/dx[f/g]=(f'g−fg')/g²
Power rule automation is the baseline. Slow differentiation here compounds in every later problem.
Common mistakes
  • Reversing quotient rule numerator order: must be (f'g−fg') not (fg'−f'g)
  • Not rewriting radicals and fractions as powers first
  • Forgetting to square the denominator in quotient rule
Write the formula before substituting. Label f, g, f', g' clearly before plugging in.
2.6 ★
2.6 — Chain Rule (Most Critical)
d/dx[f(g(x))]=f'(g(x))·g'(x) — everything in Ch.3 and Ch.4 depends on this
▾
Must be able to do
  • d/dx sin(3x²+1) = cos(3x²+1)·6x
  • d/dx e^(x²) = e^(x²)·2x
  • d/dx ln(cos x) = (1/cos x)·(−sin x)
  • d/dx (x²+1)^5 = 5(x²+1)^4·2x
  • Combined with product rule: d/dx x²·sin(3x)
Chain Rule is the foundation of implicit diff (3.1), related rates (3.4), and ALL integration techniques in Ch.7. Be honest with yourself before moving past it.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting to multiply by the inner derivative — most common error in all differentiation
  • Not identifying outer and inner functions first
  • Stopping at f'(g(x)) without multiplying by g'(x)
Protocol: What is the OUTER function? What is the INNER function? Differentiate outer (leaving inner unchanged), then multiply by derivative of inner.
3.1
3.1 — Implicit Differentiation
Treating y as function of x · dy/dx · tangent lines to implicit curves
▾
Must be able to do
  • Differentiate x²+y²=25 with respect to x
  • Handle product terms like xy using product rule
  • Find tangent line to implicitly defined curve
Requires Chain Rule fluency from 2.6. Used in related rates (3.4).
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting Chain Rule on y terms: d/dx[y²]=2y·(dy/dx) not 2y
  • Differentiating only one side of the equation
  • Not solving for dy/dx at the end
Every time you differentiate a y term, write ·(dy/dx). Then collect all dy/dx terms on one side and solve.
3.4
3.4 — Related Rates
Implicit differentiation with respect to time · diagram first · substitute last
▾
Must be able to do
  • Draw a diagram and define all variables
  • Write one equation relating the quantities
  • Differentiate both sides with respect to t
  • Substitute given values ONLY after differentiating
Common on HW exams. Applies Chain Rule and implicit differentiation to real-world problems.
Common mistakes
  • Substituting given values BEFORE differentiating — most common error
  • Not drawing a diagram first
  • Forgetting dx/dt or dy/dt on changing quantities
4-step protocol: diagram → equation → differentiate w.r.t. t → substitute values LAST.
4.1–4.3
4.1–4.3 — Curve Sketching
Sign charts for f' and f'' · extrema · concavity · asymptotes
▾
Must be able to do
  • Build sign chart for f' and f'' with all critical numbers
  • Identify intervals of increase/decrease and concavity
  • Locate inflection points where f'' changes sign
  • Classify cusps and vertical tangents from f' behavior
Combines limits at infinity (1.3), continuity (1.5), and all derivative rules into a complete picture.
Common mistakes
  • Saying increasing where f''>0 — that is concavity, not increase
  • Listing inflection points where f''=0 without checking sign actually changes
  • Confusing a hole with a vertical asymptote
Checklist: domain → intercepts → asymptotes → sign chart for f' → sign chart for f'' → sketch.
4.5
4.5 — Optimization
Objective function · constraint · reducing to one variable · verify critical point
▾
Must be able to do
  • Define all variables clearly
  • Write the objective function (what to maximize/minimize)
  • Use the constraint to eliminate one variable
  • Verify the critical point gives a max or min in context
One of the most common and difficult exam topics. Requires all previous skills plus problem-setup discipline.
Common mistakes
  • Substituting the constraint before writing the objective function
  • Not checking the nature of the critical point
  • Forgetting domain restrictions in context
4-step protocol: define variables → write objective → substitute constraint → find and verify critical point.
5.3
5.3 — U-Substitution
u=g(x) · du=g'(x)dx · rewrite entirely in u · back-substitute
▾
Must be able to do
  • Choose u correctly (usually the inner function)
  • Compute du and rewrite entire integral in u
  • Evaluate in u and substitute back to x
  • Change limits for definite integrals
U-substitution is the integration counterpart of the Chain Rule. If the Chain Rule created it, u-sub undoes it.
Common mistakes
  • Leaving a mix of x and u in the integral
  • Forgetting to change limits for definite integrals
  • Choosing u that does not simplify the integral
Look for a function and its derivative in the integrand. Let u be the inner function. Verify du matches the remaining factor.
5.6 ★
5.6 — Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
FTC Part 1: d/dx∫f(t)dt=f(x) · FTC Part 2: ∫f(x)dx=F(b)−F(a)
▾
Must be able to do
  • Apply FTC Part 1 with Chain Rule for non-x upper limit
  • Use FTC Part 2 to evaluate definite integrals exactly
  • Explain the connection between the two parts
FTC is the most important theorem in calculus. It connects differentiation and integration — two seemingly different operations.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting Chain Rule factor when upper limit is g(x) not just x
  • Not evaluating F at both limits and subtracting
  • Confusing which part of FTC to apply
Part 1: differentiate under the integral sign. Part 2: find any antiderivative F, compute F(b)−F(a).
7.2
7.2 — Integration by Parts
∫u dv=uv−∫v du · LIATE rule · tabular method
▾
Must be able to do
  • Apply integration by parts once and twice
  • Use LIATE: Logs, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential
  • Apply tabular method for ∫x²e^x dx type problems
  • Handle cyclic integrals (original integral reappears)
Integration by parts is the integration counterpart of the product rule.
Common mistakes
  • Choosing u and dv backwards — use LIATE
  • Forgetting the minus sign before ∫v du
  • Not recognizing cyclic case — let recurring integral = I, solve algebraically
LIATE: whichever appears first in the list is u. For cyclic: let I = the full integral, isolate it algebraically.
7.4
7.4 — Trig Substitution
√(a²−x²) · √(a²+x²) · √(x²−a²) · reference triangle
▾
Must be able to do
  • √(a²−x²) → x=a sinθ
  • √(a²+x²) → x=a tanθ
  • √(x²−a²) → x=a secθ
  • Complete substitution including dx, convert back using reference triangle
Handles integrals with square roots that no other technique can.
Common mistakes
  • Using the wrong substitution for the radical form
  • Forgetting to substitute dx in terms of dθ
  • Not drawing the reference triangle for back-substitution
Draw the reference triangle immediately. Label all three sides using the substitution. Use it to convert all trig back to x.
8.2
8.2 — Separation of Variables
Separating dy/dx · integrating both sides · applying initial condition
▾
Must be able to do
  • Identify separable DEs
  • Separate and integrate both sides with correct form
  • Apply initial condition to find particular solution
Primary technique for first-order DEs. Exponential growth/decay models are directly relevant to pre-med path.
Common mistakes
  • Separating incorrectly — both sides must be fully separated before integrating
  • Forgetting +C — one C overall after combining both sides
  • Not solving for y when explicit form is requested
Get all y terms and dy on one side, all x terms and dx on the other. Integrate both sides, then solve for y.
9.3–9.5
9.3–9.5 — Series Convergence Tests
Geometric · divergence test · integral test · p-series · comparison · ratio · root
▾
Must be able to do
  • Recognize geometric series: sum = a/(1−r) when |r|<1
  • Apply divergence test: if lim a_n ≠ 0, series diverges
  • Choose the appropriate test based on series form
  • Apply each test correctly and state the conclusion
Convergence tests are one of the most tested topics in Calc 2. Recognizing which test to use is the key skill.
Common mistakes
  • Concluding convergence from divergence test — it only proves divergence
  • Using ratio test when limit=1 — it is inconclusive
  • Applying comparison without verifying the inequality direction
Ratio test: best for factorials and exponentials. Integral test: when f(n) is easy to integrate. Comparison: when similar to p-series.

sin
Trigonometry
Anton 0.3 · Feeds directly into Ch.2, Ch.5, Ch.7
▾
What you need to know
Unit circle values for sin, cos, tan at 0, π/6, π/4, π/3, π/2 and their reflections. All 6 trig functions and their reciprocal relationships. Pythagorean identities: sin²x+cos²x=1, 1+tan²x=sec²x. Graphs: shape, period, and amplitude of sin, cos, tan.
SELF-CHECK
Calculus impact if weak
  • 2.5 Trig derivatives — requires knowing all 6 trig functions cold
  • 3.1 Implicit diff — trig appears in most implicit curves
  • 5.3 U-substitution — trig expressions appear constantly
  • 7.3 Trig integrals — entire section is trig identities applied to integration
  • 7.4 Trig substitution — requires fluency with all identities and inverse trig
How to fix it fast
Make a unit circle from memory — practice until it takes under 2 minutes. Memorize the 3 Pythagorean identities. Then do Anton 0.3 problems. Trig derivatives (2.5) are a separate memorization task done in Ch.2.
Memorize trig derivatives in pairs: sin/cos, tan/sec, cot/csc. Second in each pair is negative.
ln
Exponential & Logarithm Functions
Anton 0.5 · Feeds into Ch.3, Ch.5, Ch.8
▾
What you need to know
Log laws: ln(ab)=ln a+ln b, ln(a/b)=ln a−ln b, ln(a^n)=n·ln a. e^(ln x)=x and ln(e^x)=x — these are inverses. Domain: ln x requires x>0. Solve exponential and log equations, checking for extraneous solutions.
SELF-CHECK
Calculus impact if weak
  • 3.2 Log derivatives — d/dx ln(f(x))=f'(x)/f(x) requires knowing what ln is
  • 3.3 Exp derivatives — d/dx e^(f(x)) requires knowing e^x
  • 5.10 Log defined by integrals — ∫(1/x)dx=ln|x|+C
  • 8.4 First-order linear DEs — exponential growth/decay models
How to fix it fast
Read Anton 0.5. Write the 3 log laws 10 times until automatic. Do 10 equations where you must solve for x. Then do the log derivative section in Ch.3 — it will feel natural.
The single most important thing: ln(a+b) ≠ ln a + ln b. This wrong step appears in student work constantly.
f⁻¹
Inverse Functions & Inverse Trig

What you need to know
How to find f⁻¹(x) by swapping x and y. Domain/range swap when inverting. Principal ranges: arcsin [−π/2,π/2], arccos [0,π], arctan (−π/2,π/2). f⁻¹(x) ≠ 1/f(x) — these are completely different.
SELF-CHECK
Calculus impact if weak
  • 3.3 Inverse trig derivatives — d/dx arcsin x=1/√(1−x²), d/dx arctan x=1/(1+x²)
  • 7.4 Trig substitution — requires comfort with inverse trig to convert back
  • 7.5 Partial fractions — arctan appears in results
How to fix it fast
Read Anton 0.4. Focus especially on the three principal ranges — draw them on a unit circle. Memorize which range each inverse trig function uses and why. Then check the inverse trig derivative formulas in 3.3 — they follow directly.
arccos has range [0,π] — it does NOT include negative angles. This trips up most students on integration problems.

Algebra Fundamentals
Factoring · rational expressions · radicals · completing the square
▾
What you need to know
Factor any polynomial quickly: difference of squares, trinomials, grouping. Simplify rational expressions: combine fractions with different denominators. Rewrite radicals as fractional exponents. Complete the square for quadratics. These are not "Pre-cal skills" — they appear in every Calculus chapter.
SELF-CHECK
Calculus impact if weak
  • 1.2 Computing limits — 0/0 forms require factoring to resolve
  • 2.3 Power rule — requires rewriting radicals as exponents first
  • 7.5 Partial fractions — requires factoring denominators and solving systems
  • Every chapter — algebra errors compound on top of Calculus errors
How to fix it fast
This does not require a textbook. Do 20 factoring problems, 10 rational expression simplifications, and 10 radical-to-exponent conversions. Time yourself. If any take more than 60 seconds, repeat until automatic.
Rewriting before differentiating: √x = x^(1/2), ∛x = x^(1/3), 1/x^n = x^(−n). Do this before applying any rule.
Core principle — Anton First, Video Second. Read Anton first. Struggle with it. Only then open Professor Leonard.
